About Us

In the growing city of Gaborone, Botswana, two businessmen Elliot and Jaxon kept running into the same problem from very different angles. Elliot owned a small chain of pharmacies. Every day, he saw customers confused about their medications, missing doses, or coming back too late for preventable issues. Jaxon, on the other hand, ran a modest tech consultancy. His clients were businesses trying to modernize, yet he couldn’t ignore how little technology was being used to improve everyday health. They met by chance at a local networking event. Elliot mentioned how people often relied on memory or handwritten notes to manage serious health conditions. Jaxon immediately saw the gap: “Why isn’t there something simple on people’s phones for this?” That conversation turned into weeks of coffee meetings, whiteboard sketches, and late-night debates. They decided to build a company together—one that blended healthcare with digital tools. They called it *Medizen. At first, progress was slow. They had limited funding and skepticism from both industries. Healthcare professionals worried technology would complicate things, while tech investors doubted rural and semi-urban populations would adopt digital wellness tools. But Elliot insisted on starting small and practical. Their first product wasn’t flashy—it was a simple mobile app that sent medication reminders, tracked basic health data, and worked offline when internet access was unreliable. Jaxon made sure it ran smoothly even on older smartphones, which were more common across Botswana. They partnered with local clinics to test it. At one clinic, a nurse reported that patients who used the app were returning on time and managing their treatments better. That was the turning point. Word spread. They expanded the app to include features like: * Telehealth check-ins for remote patients * Wellness tips tailored to local lifestyles * A digital record system clinics could access securely What made Medizen different wasn’t just the technology—it was how deeply it understood the people using it. The app supported local languages, accounted for limited connectivity, and focused on real problems rather than trends. Within a few years, Medizen grew beyond Gaborone. It reached rural communities where healthcare access had always been a challenge. Patients who once traveled hours for basic consultations could now connect digitally. Clinics became more efficient. Preventable illnesses started declining in some areas. Investors who once hesitated began to take notice. But Elliot and Jaxon stayed grounded. They still visited clinics, spoke with users, and adjusted their platform based on real feedback. For them, success wasn’t just measured in profits—it was in healthier communities and fewer preventable emergencies. One evening, standing outside a clinic that had just gone fully digital with their system, Jaxon turned to Elliot and said, “Funny how this started with just a conversation.” Elliot smiled. “Not just a conversation. A problem we refused to ignore.” And that’s how two businessmen, from different worlds, built a company that quietly transformed health and digital wellness across Botswana.
